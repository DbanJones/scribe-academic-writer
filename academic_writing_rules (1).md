# Academic Writing Quality: Source Summaries and Implementable Rules

## 1. Source Summaries

### 1.1 Joseph Williams & Joseph Bizup - *Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace* (1990, 12th ed. 2016)

Williams, a professor at the University of Chicago who co-founded its renowned writing programme, built a unified editing system grounded in two core principles. First, if your syntax follows a prototypical structure that is simple to parse, your prose becomes immensely clearer. Second, if the syntax of your sentences mirrors the semantics of your message, readers can process meaning without mental gymnastics.

**Key contributions:**

- **Characters and actions:** Readers locate who or what a sentence is about in its subjects, and what is happening in its verbs. When writers bury action in nouns or let verbs go slack with empty helpers, they force readers to reconstruct the story from fragments.
- **Nominalizations:** Williams identifies the mechanism by which writers hide action and responsibility. Turning verbs into abstract nouns ("investigation" instead of "investigate," "implementation" instead of "implement") evacuates agency from sentences.
- **Cohesion (old-to-new):** Begin sentences with familiar information (the "topic position") and end with new, complex, important information (the "stress position"). This creates a sense of flow.
- **Concision:** A sentence is concise when it communicates its meaning in the fewest words possible without sacrificing clarity or grace.
- **Ethical dimension:** Obscure writing is often a tool for evading responsibility. The passive voice hides the agent of action ("mistakes were made" instead of "we made mistakes").

---

### 1.2 Steven Pinker - *The Sense of Style* (2014)

Pinker, a cognitive scientist and linguist at Harvard, applies findings from psychology and linguistics to explain *why* certain writing works. He distinguishes genuine rules from folklore and superstition, grounding his prescriptions in how the brain processes language.

**Key contributions:**

- **Classic prose style:** The writer acts as a guide showing the reader something in the world. The prose is a window, not a mirror. This counters the tendency in academic writing to foreground the writer's process rather than the subject.
- **The curse of knowledge:** The single best explanation for why good people write bad prose. Writers fail to bridge the gap between what they know and what their readers know. The antidote is concrete examples and vivid imagery that ground abstract ideas.
- **Minimise metadiscourse:** Excessive "verbiage about verbiage" (e.g., "In this section, we will discuss...") signals insecurity rather than helpfulness. Keep signposting minimal.
- **Syntactic tree management:** Readers hold a branching structure in memory as they parse sentences. Overloading the left branch (long subject phrases before the verb) or creating deeply nested clauses makes sentences collapse under their own weight.
- **Consistent protagonist:** Keep the spotlight on a consistent character or agent throughout a passage rather than shifting between passively affected entities.
- **Fresh wording over familiar verbiage:** Insist on concrete imagery over abstract summary.

---

### 1.3 Helen Sword - *Stylish Academic Writing* (2012)

Sword, a professor at the University of Auckland, analysed over 1,000 peer-reviewed articles across disciplines and documented a gap between how academics describe good writing and the turgid prose they actually produce. She identifies six Cs that define stylish academic writing: Communication, Craft, Creativity, Concrete language, Choice, and Courage.

**Key contributions:**

- **Three core sentence-level principles:** (1) Use concrete nouns and vivid verbs, especially when discussing abstract concepts. (2) Keep nouns and verbs close together so readers can identify "who's kicking whom." (3) Avoid weighing down sentences with extraneous words ("clutter").
- **"Zombie nouns":** Sword's memorable term (from her TED talk viewed by over 1 million people) for nominalizations that drain the life from academic prose.
- **Engaging titles:** Titles should engage the reader, inform the reader, or do both. The most effective titles resemble journalistic ones rather than purely descriptive labels.
- **Opening hooks:** Stylish writers understand that if readers are still engaged three pages in, they are committed for the long haul. A leaden first paragraph sinks a piece of prose more efficiently than anything else.
- **The CARS model (from Swales):** Creating a Research Space follows four moves: establish significance, summarise previous research, show the research gap, and present your contribution.
- **"Show and tell":** Where creative writers say "show, don't tell," academic writers should illuminate abstract concepts through practical examples and real-world applications.
- **Jargon control:** Avoid jargon except where specialised terminology is essential to the argument. Define terms when first used.

---

### 1.4 Joshua Schimel - *Writing Science* (2012)

Schimel, an ecology professor at UC Santa Barbara, applies principles of story structure to every level of scientific writing, from overall paper structure down to individual sentences. His central thesis: as a scientist, you are a professional writer.

**Key contributions:**

- **The OCAR structure:** Every scientific story has four elements: Opening (the larger problem), Challenge (the specific question), Action (the research), and Resolution (what was learned). OCAR maps onto IMRaD: Introduction = O+C, Methods/Results = A, Discussion = R.
- **The hourglass shape:** A wide opening narrows to a specific challenge and action, then widens again in the resolution. The widths of O and R must match: if O is wider than R, you overpromise and underdeliver.
- **SUCCES framework (from Heath & Heath):** Sticky ideas are Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, and Stories. The legitimate emotion in science is curiosity.
- **Story arcs at every level:** The story structure applies not only to the paper as a whole but to each section, each paragraph, and each sentence. Characters are scientific concepts; plots lie in the structure.
- **Topic and stress positions in sentences:** Whatever appears at the beginning of a sentence, readers interpret as the topic. Endings are power positions where the most important information should land.
- **Resolution links back to opening:** A good resolution steps backward through OCAR, reiterating the action, answering the challenge, and demonstrating how the opening problem has been advanced.

### 1.5 William Strunk Jr. & E.B. White - *The Elements of Style* (1918/1959, 4th ed. 2000)

The most frequently assigned text in US academic syllabuses. Strunk's original "little book" was a 43-page summation of the case for cleanliness, accuracy, and brevity. White's additions gave it broader reach.

**Key contributions (11 Principles of Composition):**

1. Choose a suitable design and stick to it
2. Make the paragraph the unit of composition
3. Use the active voice
4. Put statements in positive form
5. Use definite, specific, concrete language
6. Omit needless words
7. Avoid a succession of loose sentences
8. Express coordinate ideas in similar form (parallel construction)
9. Keep related words together
10. In summaries, keep to one tense
11. Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end

Their most famous principle, "Omit needless words," is explained thus: vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts.

---

### 1.6 William Zinsser - *On Writing Well* (1976, 30th anniversary ed. 2006)

Zinsser, a journalist and writing teacher at Yale, distilled decades of professional writing experience into four articles of faith: clarity, simplicity, brevity, and humanity.

**Key contributions:**

- **Clutter as disease:** The secret of good writing is to strip every sentence to its cleanest components. Clutter includes laborious phrases that have pushed out shorter words ("due to the fact that" instead of "because"), unnecessary adverbs, redundant adjectives, and hedge words.
- **Specific clutter targets:** Words like "facilitate" (use "ease"), "numerous" (use "many"), "sufficient" (use "enough"), "attempt" (use "try"), "individual" (use "person"), "implement" (use "do").
- **Kill qualifiers:** Prune "a bit," "a little," "sort of," "kind of," "rather," "quite," "very," "too," "pretty much." They dilute style and persuasiveness.
- **Active over passive:** The difference between active and passive verbs is one of "clarity and vigour."
- **Unity:** Maintain one mood, one pronoun perspective, and one tense within a passage.
- **Read aloud:** If you stumble or run out of breath, the sentence needs rewriting.
- **Rewriting is the essence:** Most first drafts can be cut by 50% without losing information or voice.

---

### 1.7 Graff & Birkenstein - *They Say / I Say* (2006)

This book has essentially defined academic argumentation by identifying its key rhetorical moves. The central insight is that academic writing is fundamentally a dialogue with existing scholarship.

**Key contributions:**

- **"They Say / I Say" structure:** Effective academic arguments begin by summarising what others have said, then introduce the writer's own contribution in response.
- **Templates as scaffolding:** Rhetorical templates ("While X argues..., I contend...", "Although it is true that..., it does not follow that...") are not formulaic but represent the deep moves of academic discourse.
- **Entering the conversation:** Academic writing should always position itself in relation to existing debate rather than existing in a vacuum.

## 2. Implementable Rules for an Academic Writing Tool

The following rules are derived from convergent principles across all sources. They are organised from sentence-level mechanics up to document-level structure, making them suitable for implementation as automated checks or guided suggestions.

---

### Category A: Word-Level Rules

**A1. Flag Nominalizations (Williams, Sword, Schimel, Pinker)**
Detect nouns derived from verbs (typically ending in -tion, -ment, -ness, -ance, -ence, -ity, -ing used as noun) that serve as the subject of an empty verb ("make," "do," "perform," "conduct," "carry out," "have," "give," "take," "is," "are").

*Trigger:* "The implementation of the policy was carried out by the committee"
*Suggestion:* "The committee implemented the policy"

**Exceptions** (Williams): Nominalizations are acceptable when they (a) refer to a previously mentioned concept, (b) replace an awkward "the fact that" construction, (c) name a concept so familiar to readers it functions as a character, or (d) serve as the object of the verb.

**A2. Flag Clutter Words and Phrases (Zinsser, Strunk & White)**
Maintain a dictionary of wordy phrases and their concise replacements:

| Cluttered | Concise |
|-----------|---------|
| due to the fact that | because |
| in order to | to |
| at this point in time | now |
| in the event that | if |
| it is important to note that | (delete or rephrase) |
| a large number of | many |
| has the ability to | can |
| in spite of the fact that | although / despite |
| on the basis of | based on / from |
| with regard to | about / regarding |
| prior to | before |
| subsequent to | after |
| in close proximity to | near |
| is indicative of | indicates / suggests |
| take into consideration | consider |

**A3. Flag Unnecessary Qualifiers (Zinsser)**
Detect hedging words that dilute meaning: "a bit," "a little," "sort of," "kind of," "rather," "quite," "very," "too," "pretty much," "somewhat," "relatively," "fairly."

*Suggestion:* "Consider whether this qualifier adds precision or merely dilutes the claim."

**A4. Flag Inflated Vocabulary (Zinsser, Pinker)**
Detect Latinate/complex words where simpler alternatives exist:

| Inflated | Simple |
|----------|--------|
| utilize | use |
| facilitate | help / ease |
| endeavour | try |
| sufficient | enough |
| numerous | many |
| commence | begin / start |
| terminate | end |
| approximately | about |
| methodology | method |
| functionality | function |
| conceptualize | think / imagine |
| operationalize | apply / put into practice |
| problematize | question / challenge |

**Note:** In some academic contexts, the more specific term carries genuine additional meaning (e.g., "methodology" when discussing systematic approaches vs. a single "method"). The tool should allow users to confirm intentional use.

**A5. Flag Redundant Adverb-Verb Pairs (Zinsser)**
Detect adverbs whose meaning is already conveyed by the verb: "completely finished," "totally destroyed," "absolutely essential," "carefully scrutinised."

### Category B: Sentence-Level Rules

**B1. Characters as Subjects (Williams, Pinker, Schimel)**
Check whether the grammatical subject of each sentence names a real character (a person, group, institution, concept, or phenomenon that acts). Flag sentences where the subject is an abstract nominalization or a vague "it" / "there" construction.

*Trigger:* "There is a need for further investigation of the phenomenon"
*Suggestion:* "Researchers need to investigate the phenomenon further"

**B2. Actions in Verbs (Williams, Sword, Schimel)**
Check whether the main verb of the sentence describes a genuine action. Flag sentences where the main verb is an "empty" verb (be, have, make, do, give, take, provide, perform, conduct) paired with a nominalization that carries the real action.

*Trigger:* "The researchers performed an analysis of the data"
*Suggestion:* "The researchers analysed the data"

**B3. Active Voice Preference (Strunk & White, Zinsser, Sword)**
Flag passive constructions and suggest active alternatives. However, passive voice is acceptable when: (a) the agent is unknown or irrelevant, (b) the agent is less important than the action/object, (c) maintaining topic continuity requires it, or (d) the discipline conventionally uses passive in certain sections (e.g., Methods).

*Severity:* Suggestion, not error. Display as "Consider active voice: [suggested rewrite]."

**B4. Subject-Verb Distance (Williams, Sword)**
Measure the number of words between the main subject and its verb. Flag when this exceeds approximately 10-12 words, as readers lose track of the sentence's framework.

*Trigger:* "The analysis of the data collected from three separate field sites over the course of two years reveals..."
*Suggestion:* "Move the subject and verb closer together. Consider: 'The analysis reveals... [details about data collection]' or restructure."

**B5. Sentence Length Variation (Sword, Pinker)**
Calculate the standard deviation of sentence lengths in each paragraph/section. Flag passages where all sentences fall within a narrow band (e.g., all 20-30 words), suggesting variation for rhythm and readability.

Also flag individual sentences exceeding approximately 40-50 words as candidates for splitting.

**B6. Left-Branching Overload (Pinker)**
Detect sentences with long introductory phrases or multiple subordinate clauses before the main verb. When the pre-verb material exceeds roughly 15-20 words, suggest restructuring.

*Trigger:* "Despite the fact that numerous studies conducted over the past decade in various institutional settings across multiple countries have suggested otherwise, the policy..."
*Suggestion:* "Consider moving the main clause earlier and placing qualifying information afterward."

**B7. Parallel Construction (Strunk & White)**
When coordinating items in a list or series (whether in-text or as bullet points), check that all items follow the same grammatical form (all noun phrases, all verb phrases, all clauses of the same type).

*Trigger:* "The study aims to analyse energy consumption, modelling future scenarios, and the development of policy recommendations."
*Suggestion:* "The study aims to analyse energy consumption, model future scenarios, and develop policy recommendations."

**B8. Positive Form (Strunk & White)**
Flag negative constructions where a positive statement would be more direct:

| Negative | Positive |
|----------|----------|
| not honest | dishonest |
| not important | unimportant / trivial |
| did not remember | forgot |
| not the same | different |
| not unlike | similar |

**B9. End-Weight / Stress Position (Williams, Schimel, Strunk & White)**
Check whether sentences place their most important or complex information at the end (the "stress position"). Flag sentences that bury the key point in the middle and trail off with minor qualifications.

### Category C: Paragraph-Level Rules

**C1. Topic Sentences (Williams, Schimel)**
Check that each paragraph begins with a clear topic sentence that signals what the paragraph is about. The first 7-8 words should orient the reader.

**C2. Old-to-New Information Flow (Williams, Pinker)**
Within each paragraph, check that each sentence begins with information familiar from the previous sentence or context (the "topic") and ends with new information (the "stress"). Flag sentences that introduce entirely new concepts at the beginning without any link to what precedes them.

*Heuristic:* Check whether key terms from the end of sentence N appear near the beginning of sentence N+1.

**C3. Paragraph Unity (Strunk & White, Zinsser)**
Each paragraph should develop a single idea. Flag paragraphs that introduce more than one distinct topic or that exceed approximately 200-250 words without a clear through-line.

**C4. Paragraph-Level Story Arc (Schimel)**
Each paragraph should have its own micro-structure: a topic sentence (opening), development (action), and a concluding point (resolution). Flag paragraphs that lack a clear concluding thought or that simply stop mid-development.

---

### Category D: Section and Document-Level Rules

**D1. OCAR Completeness Check (Schimel)**
For research papers, check that the document contains all four OCAR elements:
- **Opening:** Does the introduction establish the larger problem and its significance?
- **Challenge:** Does the introduction narrow to specific research questions or hypotheses?
- **Action:** Do the methods and results describe what was done?
- **Resolution:** Does the discussion/conclusion resolve the challenge and connect back to the opening?

Flag missing or weak elements.

**D2. Hourglass Shape Verification (Schimel)**
Check scope alignment: does the resolution address the same scope of problem established in the opening? Flag cases where the introduction promises broad impact but the conclusion discusses only narrow findings, or vice versa.

**D3. Introduction Funnel (Schimel, Swales CARS model)**
Check that the introduction follows a narrowing trajectory:
1. General problem / significance (wide)
2. Relevant prior work
3. Gap in knowledge
4. Specific research question (narrow)

Flag introductions that jump straight to specifics without establishing context, or that remain at a general level without narrowing.

**D4. "They Say / I Say" Positioning (Graff & Birkenstein)**
Check that the argument is positioned relative to existing scholarship. Flag sections (particularly introductions and literature reviews) that present claims without referencing what others have said. Look for positioning language: "While X argues...", "Building on...", "In contrast to...", "Extending..."

**D5. Metadiscourse Audit (Pinker)**
Flag excessive signposting and metadiscourse. Phrases like "In this section, we will discuss...", "As mentioned earlier...", "It is important to note that...", "The remainder of this paper is organised as follows..." are acceptable in moderation but should not dominate. Count metadiscourse sentences as a proportion of total and flag when it exceeds roughly 5-8%.

**D6. Jargon Density Check (Sword, Pinker)**
Count the number of field-specific technical terms per paragraph. Flag paragraphs where jargon density is high and no definitions or explanations have been provided for terms introduced for the first time.

**D7. Engaging Title Check (Sword)**
Evaluate whether the title:
- Contains only a descriptive label ("An Analysis of X") -- suggest adding an informative or engaging element
- Uses an effective two-part structure (engaging hook : descriptive subtitle)
- Is unnecessarily long (suggest trimming)

**D8. Opening Hook Assessment (Sword)**
Check the first paragraph for engagement features: an opening question, a striking fact, a relevant anecdote, a bold claim, or a clear statement of why the topic matters. Flag openings that begin with generic background ("Since the dawn of time..." or "X has received increasing attention...").

---

### Category E: Clarity and Readability Metrics

**E1. Readability Score (derived from all sources)**
Calculate readability using Flesch-Kincaid or similar, but calibrate for academic audiences. Academic writing should aim for a grade level appropriate to its audience (typically 12-16 for journal articles). Flag text that scores significantly above this range, as it may indicate unnecessarily complex syntax rather than sophisticated ideas.

**E2. Average Sentence Length**
Calculate average words per sentence. Academic writing typically ranges from 15-25 words per sentence. Flag sections where the average consistently exceeds 30, suggesting the need for sentence splitting.

**E3. Passive Voice Proportion**
Calculate the percentage of passive constructions. Flag when this exceeds approximately 30-40% of all sentences (adjustable by discipline and section type: Methods sections may legitimately have higher passive use).

**E4. Nominalization Density**
Count nominalizations per 100 words. Flag passages where density exceeds roughly 4-5 per 100 words as likely to feel abstract and difficult to parse.

**E5. "To Be" Verb Frequency**
Count forms of "to be" (is, are, was, were, been, being) as a proportion of all verbs. A high proportion (above roughly 40%) often correlates with passive voice, nominalizations, and weak sentence construction.

---

### Category F: Academic Conventions

**F1. Hedging Appropriateness**
Academic writing requires hedging ("suggests," "indicates," "may," "appears to") when claims are probabilistic. Flag absolute claims about empirical findings ("proves," "demonstrates conclusively") where hedging would be more appropriate. Conversely, flag excessive hedging on well-established facts.

**F2. Citation Integration**
Flag sentences that consist entirely of citations strung together without the author's synthesis or commentary. Effective academic writing weaves citations into the author's own argument rather than listing them.

**F3. Signpost Consistency**
If the introduction promises to address specific topics in a specific order, verify that the paper follows through in the same sequence. Flag mismatches between promised and delivered structure.

**F4. Tense Consistency (Strunk & White)**
Check for tense consistency within sections:
- Literature review: past tense or present perfect for previous findings
- Methods: past tense for what was done
- Results: past tense for findings
- Discussion: present tense for interpretations and implications

Flag unwarranted tense shifts within a single paragraph.

## 3. Implementation Priority

For an MVP academic writing tool, prioritise rules by impact and feasibility:

**Tier 1 (High Impact, Feasible to Automate):**
- A1: Nominalization detection
- A2: Clutter phrase replacement
- B3: Passive voice flagging
- B4: Subject-verb distance
- E1-E5: Readability metrics
- B5: Sentence length variation

**Tier 2 (High Impact, Moderate Complexity):**
- B1: Characters as subjects
- B2: Actions in verbs
- C1: Topic sentence detection
- A3: Qualifier detection
- A4: Inflated vocabulary
- B7: Parallel construction

**Tier 3 (High Impact, Requires Deeper NLP/LLM):**
- C2: Old-to-new information flow
- D1: OCAR completeness
- D4: "They Say / I Say" positioning
- D5: Metadiscourse audit
- D6: Jargon density with definition tracking
- D3: Introduction funnel structure
